# Factual Advocacy Summary for Counsel

## Executive Summary
The document trail from **June 2023 through the end of the 1590 Harrison sale process in late May and early June 2024** supports a strong, source-anchored narrative that Melissa remained financially exposed on real estate, accounts, and debt while Jesse continued to press for control of property-related income and delayed or failed to complete the steps needed to unwind that exposure. The court file shows the case was filed on **June 8, 2023**, a case-management order entered on **June 9, 2023**, the Memorandum of Understanding was filed on **December 5, 2023**, the Separation Agreement was filed on **February 2, 2024**, and the **Decree of Dissolution of Marriage** entered on **February 6, 2024**. But the end of the marriage did not end the financial entanglement. The record instead shows continuing mortgage disputes, reimbursement disputes, account entanglement, Airbnb and address liability tied to 1590, a quitclaim dispute after decree, and a delayed, contentious path to sale.

The strongest current factual narrative is that Melissa remained financially trapped until the `1590 Harrison` sale closed and cleanup followed. By **July 18, 2023**, she was already telling counsel that she needed to move in with her father in order to pay the mortgages she held while Jesse managed the rental and Airbnb. By **July 24, 2023**, counsel was warning that the ISC was only a deadline-setting conference and that nothing financial would be addressed there. By **August 23, 2023**, the court dates being offered were a **January 4, 2024 pretrial conference** and a **February 27, 2024 permanent orders hearing**, and on **August 28, 2023** counsel warned that even a temporary-orders hearing might take **at least 45 days**. That procedural timeline matters because the same record shows Jesse continuing to assert control over rental or Airbnb income while Melissa remained tied to mortgages, title, account liability, and debt verification. The same record also shows that the SBA loan was in Jesse's name, that Melissa was expected to reimburse him without being given the lender-side SBA file, that Jesse was supposed to provide monthly statements while she remained on 1590 title and mortgage, and that the workspace still does not contain those monthly SBA statements or the origination documents. The present file therefore supports a strong advocacy argument that the combination of Jesse's non-compliance and the court's delayed ability to provide practical interim relief allowed the financial damage to deepen over many months.
