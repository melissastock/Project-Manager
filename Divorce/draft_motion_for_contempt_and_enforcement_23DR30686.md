INTRODUCTION

1. This is a post-decree enforcement motion arising from Respondent's repeated failure to perform specific
obligations imposed by the parties' Separation Agreement and incorporated into the Decree of Dissolution.

2. The motion is not based on generalized dissatisfaction with the divorce outcome. It is based on specific
acts of noncompliance that can be tied to the parties' written agreement, Respondent's own admissions,
bank records, correspondence, and other contemporaneous documents.

3. The strongest presently documented enforcement claim is Respondent's failure to pay the 1590 Harrison
mortgage obligations he admitted owing in writing on May 2, 2024, in the amount of $14,566.98 for the
January through May 2024 period, together with the separate November 2023 reimbursement obligation
expressly stated in the Separation Agreement.

4. The current record also supports enforcement relief based on Respondent's failure to timely complete
the assumption / refinance / sale-related steps concerning 1590 Harrison, failure to timely cooperate with
deed and related property documents, failure to provide certain financial and loan-related documentation,
and delay-related conduct that increased Petitioner's fees and prolonged her financial exposure.

5. Petitioner seeks primarily remedial contempt and enforcement relief, including entry of judgment for
currently provable sums, production of documents still in Respondent's possession or control, and an
award of attorney fees and costs under both general enforcement principles and the express fee-shifting
and indemnification language of the Separation Agreement.

JURISDICTION AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

6. This Court entered a Decree of Dissolution of Marriage on February 6, 2024.

7. The Decree incorporated the parties' Separation Agreement, thereby rendering the Agreement
enforceable through this Court's post-decree contempt and enforcement powers.

8. This Court retains jurisdiction to enforce its Decree and all obligations incorporated therein.

9. The parties' case is Denver County District Court Case No. 2023DR30686, Division 311.

RELEVANT BACKGROUND

10. During the marriage and dissolution period, Petitioner was the borrower on the mortgage obligations
at issue and remained financially exposed on the 1590 Harrison loan even after the property was allocated
to Respondent under the Separation Agreement.

11. The property located at 1590 Harrison St., Denver, Colorado, was allocated to Respondent under the
Separation Agreement, subject to obligations requiring him to assume, refinance, or sell the property and,
while the loan remained in Petitioner's name, to make the required monthly mortgage payments.

12. The property located at 3904 E. 16th Ave., Denver, Colorado, was allocated to Petitioner.

13. The Court's enforcement authority is implicated because Petitioner's financial exposure on 1590 did
not end when the property was allocated to Respondent; it continued so long as Respondent failed to carry
out the steps required by the Separation Agreement and Decree.

ORDERED DUTIES

14. Under the Separation Agreement, Respondent was awarded the 1590 Harrison property, but
Petitioner's name was to remain on title and / or mortgage obligations until the loan issue was resolved by
assumption, refinance, or sale as set forth in the Agreement.

15. The Separation Agreement required Respondent, by November 21, 2023, to apply to assume the 1590
loan and remove Petitioner from the loan, to provide Petitioner with relevant lender-side documents, to
notify Petitioner if assumption was rejected, to proceed to refinance within the contractually specified time
if necessary, and to list the property for sale if the loan issue could not be timely resolved.

16. The Separation Agreement further required Respondent, while the 1590 loan remained in Petitioner's
name, to pay the monthly mortgage obligation by transfer to Petitioner no later than the 15th of each
month.

17. The Separation Agreement states that Respondent would be responsible for late fees associated with
missed or delayed 1590 mortgage payments.

18. The Separation Agreement also contains an express reimbursement obligation in Petitioner's favor for
November 2023 in the amount of $2,929.32.

19. The Separation Agreement awarded Petitioner the 3904 property and 100% of rental income generated
from that property and required Respondent not to interfere with Petitioner's renting of the property and
to execute documents necessary to effectuate the parties' property arrangements.

20. The Separation Agreement required timely execution of deeds and other carry-out documents.

21. The Separation Agreement further contemplated production of certain financial and loan-related
information, including SBA-loan-related monthly statements while Petitioner remained exposed on title or
mortgage obligations tied to 1590.

22. Section 8.L of the Separation Agreement provides, in substance, that if one party fails to perform the
Agreement, that party must indemnify and hold the other harmless from resulting expenses, and that the
successful enforcing party is entitled to reasonable attorney fees and costs.

FACTUAL BASIS

23. Respondent had knowledge of the Court's orders and the obligations described above. He was a party
to the case, was represented by counsel, participated in mediation and settlement discussions, and signed
the operative resolution documents.

24. On November 28, 2023, while the Separation Agreement was still in circulation in draft form,
Petitioner told counsel that Respondent had recently told her he could not assume the 1590 loan and was
going to sell the property.

25. On November 28, 2023, Petitioner also advised counsel that she still needed a way to track SBA
payments and did not yet have the account number she had requested.

26. On November 30, 2023, Petitioner's counsel sent the draft Separation Agreement to Respondent's
counsel for comments and redlines.

27. On December 4, 2023, Respondent's counsel acknowledged delay and stated he would follow up with
Respondent regarding the issues and the draft Separation Agreement.

28. On December 13, 2023, Respondent's counsel stated that Respondent had recently suffered an injury
and that this had delayed review of the Separation Agreement.

29. On December 13, 2023, Petitioner's counsel responded in writing: "We have been more than patient.
This is increasing my client's attorneys fees."

30. Respondent's side still did not provide substantive redlines until January 2, 2024, more than a month
after the draft had been transmitted.

31. On October 16, 2023, before decree and while the mortgage crisis was already ongoing, Petitioner sent
her counsel a written request titled "Response Requested: Protecting Mortgages/Assets," asking what she
could legally do immediately to protect herself financially and stating that her goal was to ensure rental
and Airbnb income went to the mortgages first.

32. In that October 16, 2023 communication, Petitioner warned that the 3904 payment was late, that the
account would be short without intervention, that she was trying to avoid foreclosure, and that if
Respondent continued to have unfettered access to the money she would remain in the same position on a
monthly basis.

33. On December 7 and 8, 2023, Petitioner wrote counsel that she needed a plan so that she would not
continue to carry Respondent's mortgage or go into foreclosure if Respondent could not sell or pay for
1590, and asked for protection requiring repayment of missed payments and late fees by the date of sale.

34. On December 4, 2023, Petitioner also sent her parents a written financial-help summary listing
approximately $15,100 in December expenses, including attorney fees, rent, deposit, mortgage, roof
expense, and rental fixes, against substantially lower projected income, and requested support.

35. In that same December 4, 2023 communication, Petitioner stated that January would only be manageable
if unexpected expenses did not arise and if Jesse paid his mortgage.

36. Those December 2023 records are consistent with a financial state in which ordinary recovery had
already materially broken down, with Petitioner relying on family loans and other support while still
carrying housing and mortgage-related obligations.

37. On January 4, 2024, in connection with the pre-trial conference, Petitioner sent counsel a detailed
contemporaneous summary describing, among other things, Respondent's failure to timely address the 1590
loan issue, the continuing mortgage and housing pressure, the dispute regarding rental-income use, and the
prejudice caused by delay.

38. On February 13, 2024, after entry of Decree, Petitioner's counsel wrote Respondent's counsel that
Respondent was refusing to sign the quitclaim deed for Petitioner's house and requested immediate
cooperation.

39. On March 25, 2024, Petitioner reported to counsel that Respondent had not paid the mortgage since
January 2024 and was still delaying the listing and sale process. On that same date, Respondent also wrote
in the sale-related email chain that he needed flexibility on pricing and did not want automatic weekly
price reductions, instead proposing that the listing begin at $729,000.

40. On April 6, 2024, Respondent circulated a further sale-related email concerning remaining work at
1590 and possible cash-back-at-closing or price-reduction strategies, including a potential $10,000 buyer-
facing concession structure.

41. On April 26, 2024 and again through May 3, 2024, the 1590 transaction coordinator requested the
required disclosures and due-diligence documents, and Respondent ultimately represented that he was only
providing the documents then in his possession while identifying categories of documents he did not have.

42. On May 2, 2024, Respondent provided a written calculation showing that the amount owed to
Petitioner for January through May 2024 mortgage support, net of water offsets he claimed, was
$14,566.98.

43. That May 2, 2024 calculation is a party admission.

44. The 1590 property did not close until May 29, 2024.

45. The May 29, 2024 settlement statement for 1590 reflects a sale price of $660,000.00, a seller credit
of $10,200.00, payoff to Freedom Mortgage of $409,219.55 including $111.89 in late charges, and net
proceeds of $196,178.25 to Respondent and $11,450.85 to Petitioner. The settlement statement does not
reflect a separate line item disbursing the admitted $14,566.98 arrears amount to Petitioner.

46. During the period in which Respondent was supposed to be paying the 1590 mortgage and moving the
loan issue toward resolution, Petitioner's records reflect that she was making mortgage-related and
SBA-related payments from her own accounts and using her own financial resources to stabilize the
situation.

47. The current file also contains substantial contemporaneous evidence that Petitioner sought emergency
or temporary court intervention as early as August 2023 regarding housing, rental-income allocation,
mortgage protection, and financial control issues, including her August 30, 2023 written response to
counsel's temporary-orders questions.

48. In that August 30, 2023 response, Petitioner reported that one mortgage had been paid through joint CU
Denver checking, that another had been paid through a Fidelity IRA early-distribution transfer into SSFCU
savings, and that September had not yet been paid for either property.

49. Petitioner further reported that rental income and Airbnb income flowed into CU Denver Basic
Checking and requested that such funds be applied to mortgages and necessary household bills.

50. Separate records from December 2023 also show that Petitioner cooperated in initiating transfer of
Respondent's Verizon line, that the transfer expired without completion, and that Respondent was still
seeking to preserve Airbnb income from arrangements and licensing structures that remained in Petitioner's
name. Petitioner offers this evidence as further context for the pattern of leaving liabilities in Petitioner's
name while preserving practical benefits for Respondent.
